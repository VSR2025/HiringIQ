Before Everything · Recruiter Screening
Start With the Recruiter
Human coaching → AI audits for inadvertent bias
The Human Design

Before the recruiter spoke to a single candidate, the hiring manager and recruiter sat down together. Not a briefing — a conversation. They co-defined which signals actually predict success in this role and which ones feel like signals but aren't. The recruiter shaped the list as much as she received it.

Her job was never to screen for fit. It was to surface evidence. Her written rationale after every screening call fed directly into panel design — shaping which signals the panel needed to prioritise.

Where AI Enters

AI was not in the screening conversation. It had no role there.

After each call, the recruiter ran her own written notes through AI — not to rewrite them, but to audit them. The prompt checked for language that might reflect inadvertent bias: conclusions without evidence, comparative language, or impressions untethered to anything the candidate actually said or did.

How AI was used
The prompt
Read these recruiter notes from a screening call.
Flag anything that:
  • Draws a conclusion without citing what the candidate actually said or did.
  • Uses comparative language — better than, more polished than, unlike others — without evidence.
  • Describes an impression or feeling with no behavioural anchor.
Do not rewrite the notes. Do not assess the candidate. Just show me where the evidence is missing so I can decide what to do with it.
The guardrail
AI does not assess the candidate — ever. It audits the recruiter's own language for gaps between impression and evidence. The recruiter decides what to do with every flag. Her rationale, not AI's, goes to the hiring manager.
Impact
The recruiter arrived at panel design as a contributor, not a gatekeeper. Her signal was clean — and the hiring manager trusted it.

Before Interviews · Role Clarity
Skip the job description.
Write a letter instead.
Your thinking → AI sharpens what you already mean
The Human Design

Write to the person you hope to hire — one year in. Describe what they built, what they changed, what made you proud. That letter becomes your hiring standard.

Not a list of requirements. A picture of success. It forces clarity that a job description never demands — because you're writing to a person, not filling a template.

Where AI Enters

AI reads your letter for what humans consistently miss — vague language disguised as clarity. Words like "visionary" and "great EQ" feel specific when you write them. They mean different things to every person in the room.

AI translates each one into a specific, observable behaviour an interviewer can actually listen for.

How AI was used
The prompt
You're a hiring clarity expert coaching me through this.
I've written a future-state letter describing the person I want to hire, one year in. Read it for words that feel specific but mean different things to different people.
For each one:
  • Give me the observable behaviour it should mean. Something an interviewer can actually listen for, not just nod at.
  • Tell me why that word is a problem. Help me see it, not just fix it.
  • Give me a concrete example. Something that sounds like a real person in a real situation — not a textbook definition.
Be direct. If you spot a contradiction in what I'm asking for, call it out.
Success looks like this: I should be able to hand each observable behaviour directly to an interviewer and they'd know exactly what to listen for.
The guardrail
AI does not author intent. It sharpens what the human already means. The hiring manager owns what "good" looks like — AI makes sure they've actually said it in a way that survives a panel of five different people.
Impact
Two words on a page became criteria an interviewer could actually use. That's the whole point.
02
Interview Design · Panel Design
Build a Panel Where Every Interviewer Tests Something Different
Your human design → AI audits for gaps and redundancy
The Human Design

The recruiter's screening rationale arrived before the panel was assembled — her read on the candidate shaped which signals the panel needed to cover. The hiring manager anchored the panel from there, owning the signals only they could evaluate: overall trajectory, strategic fit, the read that comes from being accountable for the hire. The cross-functional interviewers filled everything else, each assigned a distinct signal the hiring manager couldn't objectively own themselves.

The team the new hire would work with had a seat — not as a formality, but because their read on collaboration mattered. The functional team could sit in if needed. Not as a separate loop. As observers with a voice.

Coordination happened async. No discussion before each interviewer filed independent notes. Groupthink was designed out, not hoped away.

Where AI Enters

Once the panel was mapped, AI audited it for two failure modes that are invisible until it's too late: redundancy — two interviewers testing the same signal — and blind spots — competencies nobody was covering at all.

A coverage map made every signal visible and owned. If AI flagged an overlap, the panel was redesigned before the first interview — not discovered in the debrief when it was too late to do anything about it.

How AI was used
The prompt
Review this interview panel against the role's required signals.
Flag:
  • Any signal being tested more than once across interviewers.
  • Any required signal with no interviewer assigned to it.
For each issue, suggest a specific reassignment — don't just name the problem.
The guardrail
AI flags redundancy and gaps — it does not reassign interviewers. The hiring manager reviews the audit and makes the call. Panel changes require a human decision, not an algorithm.
Impact
Every interviewer walked in with a distinct purpose. The candidate felt it. The panel felt it. No wasted round, no missed signal.
03
In the Room · The Interview
Stop Taking Notes.
Start Listening.
Your human design → AI captures so you stay present
The Human Design

The interviewer's only job in the room is to be present. Eye contact. Active listening. Running a real problem the team was actively working on — not a hypothetical. Not taking notes.

Candidates were informed at the start of the process that interviews would be transcribed, and consented before scheduling. No exceptions.

No interviewer discussed what they heard before filing their independent written assessment. That discipline was non-negotiable.

Where AI Enters

Transcription runs in the background. Verbatim. No interpretation, no summary, no flags.

After the interview, the interviewer reads the transcript to anchor their written assessment in what was actually said — not what they remember.

How AI was used
The guardrail
AI captures only — it does not evaluate, score, or flag candidate responses. Interpretation belongs to the interviewer, after the fact, with the transcript as their reference.
Impact
The interviewer was fully present. The candidate had their full attention — and felt it.
04
After the Interview · The Debrief
Make Every Interviewer Defend Their Opinion With Evidence
Your human design → AI reads across all transcripts before the room convenes
The Human Design

Every interviewer filed their written assessment independently — rating plus evidence — before any debrief conversation. If you couldn't cite a moment from the transcript, it didn't count.

No hallway conversations. No "what did you think?" before notes were in. The debrief was a comparison of independent reads, not a negotiation shaped by whoever spoke first.

Where AI Enters

AI read across all transcripts and all assessments together — something no single interviewer could do. It surfaced patterns invisible to anyone who only saw one conversation.

One interviewer noted answers felt rehearsed. Another flagged every response pivoted to team credit. A third observed no concrete example was ever given unprompted. Individually — weak signals. Together — a pattern. AI connected them before the debrief started.

AI also audited each written assessment for unsupported opinions. "Too aggressive" with no transcript reference got flagged. The hiring manager reviewed every flag before the room convened.

The human override — when AI is right and the human overrules anyway
Key Moment
AI pattern report
Clean scorecard
All four interviewers rated this candidate positively. Every assessment is anchored to transcript evidence. No unsupported opinions flagged.
Pattern noted
Every answer was structurally complete. No response was given without a framework. No example was offered unprompted — all were given when directly asked.
The human decision
Interviewer flags — no transcript support
"Too scripted. Too managed. Nothing felt spontaneous. In a role that needs someone who can think in a messy room — I'm a no."
Hiring manager's call
AI flagged no transcript evidence for this. The interviewer acknowledged that. The no stood anyway — because some signals only exist in the room. That's not bias. That's judgment.
How AI was used
The prompt
You have all interview transcripts and all written assessments for this candidate.
Do two things:
  • Read across all transcripts for patterns no single interviewer would see — consistency, contradictions, structural behaviours that repeat.
  • Read each written assessment and flag any opinion that has no supporting evidence in the transcript. Name the claim and note what's missing.
Do not make a hire/no-hire recommendation. Surface what the humans need to see before they decide.
The guardrail
AI does not make the call — ever. It surfaces patterns and flags unsupported opinions. The hiring manager decides what to do with every flag. Including overriding it.
Impact
The debrief was a conversation about evidence, not a negotiation between opinions. And when a human overrode the data — they did it with eyes open.
